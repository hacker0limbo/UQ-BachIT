    Life on a Convict Ship

    Convicts were housed below deck on the prison deck and were confined behind bars. They had to sleep on hammocks and were rarely allowed time up on the deck to exercise and have fresh air.

    A surgeon superintendent was employed to care for the prisoners’ health and a Religious Instructor attended their education. Warders maintained order.

    Convicts were forced to follow a strict set of rules. Cursing, shouting, fighting, selling, exchanging or giving away clothes were strictly forbidden. Prisoners were not allowed to converse with guards or members of the ship’s crew on any occasion and thievery of any of the ships stores would be punished severely.

    Life on a Convict Ship

    Convicts were housed below deck on the prison deck and were confined behind bars. They had to sleep on hammocks and were rarely allowed time up on the deck to exercise and have fresh air.

    A surgeon superintendent was employed to care for the prisoners’ health and a Religious Instructor attended their education. Warders maintained order.

    Convicts were forced to follow a strict set of rules. Cursing, shouting, fighting, selling, exchanging or giving away clothes were strictly forbidden. Prisoners were not allowed to converse with guards or members of the ship’s crew on any occasion and thievery of any of the ships stores would be punished severely.

    Breaking the rules

    There were over 200 rules that convicts had to follow, and the punishment for their breakage was often brutal and severe. Primary forms of punishment included:

    • Solitary confinement
    • Being forced to wear leg irons
    • The treadmill
    • Flogging

    Solitary Confinement

    The barracks had ten solitary confinement cells, which were used for convicts who had returned to the Barracks late, drunk or for committing other minor crimes. These cells were small, cramped and dark (with no windows). At different times of year, these cells could be either very hot or very cold.

    Breaking the rules

    Convicts might have been left in these cells for up to three weeks. During this time they were only given bread and water.

    Leg-irons

    More serious crimes resulting in convicts being forced to wear leg irons. These were very heavy (weighing approximately 18 kilograms) and very uncomfortable. When walking the irons would rub against the convicts ankles, becoming very painful.

    Sometimes convicts were forced to wear them for months.

    These irons were fixed around convicts ankles by a blacksmith. They had no key to take them off instead a blacksmith had to hammer out the rivet while the convict was still wearing them.

    The Treadmill

    The treadmill was a machine powered by convicts walking continuously, to power a wheel. It was hard punishment and men walking on it had to power it all day. They walked 40 minutes at a time, resting only for twenty minute spurts in between.

    Flogging

    For even more serious crimes, a convict could be flogged with a cat-o’-nine tails. However, this punishment had to be approved by a magistrate of a court of the Governor. Convicts at the barracks were forced to watch all floggings.

    Floggings could be the punishment for many different crimes. In 1883:

    • John Dennin was given 25 lashes for not doing his work
    • John Carrli was given 36 lashes for pretending to be sick
    • Alexander Somers was given 25 lashes for leaving the barracks without permission
    • Daniel Dicken was given 50 lashes for disobeying orders

    What did convicts eat?

    In 1820, the convicts living at the Hyde Park Barracks would have been woken at sunrise by the ringing of a bell in the yard. They got out of their hammocks, went downstairs and were given breakfast – a salty meat stew sometimes with a few vegetables like cabbage, onion, potato and turnip.

    The stew was cooked in a large pot that could hold 280 litres, and each man was served the same amount of meat, 227 grams (half a pound). They were also given bread. The men sat together in groups of six (known as ‘messes’), and one man had the job of giving out the food and making sure every man got the same amount.

    What did convicts eat?

    In 1820, the convicts living at the Hyde Park Barracks would have been woken at sunrise by the ringing of a bell in the yard. They got out of their hammocks, went downstairs and were given breakfast – a salty meat stew sometimes with a few vegetables like cabbage, onion, potato and turnip.

    The stew was cooked in a large pot that could hold 280 litres, and each man was served the same amount of meat, 227 grams (half a pound). They were also given bread. The men sat together in groups of six (known as ‘messes’), and one man had the job of giving out the food and making sure every man got the same amount.

    The Deputy Superintendent would keep a close eye on the men preparing the food to make sure it was properly cooked and that each convict was given the correct amount. Later in the day, the men were given more food to eat.

    By 1826, convicts at the barracks were eating something different for breakfast – a plain-tasting porridge called ‘hominy’. It was made from corn and was boiled over a fire.
